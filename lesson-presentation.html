📚 Subject-Verb Agreement

Master the foundation of clear, professional writing

💡 Why Master This Skill?

Mastering subject-verb agreement makes your writing sound confident and professional — in emails, essays, and everyday conversations.

👋 Why This Lesson Matters for Your Writing and Speaking

Subject-verb agreement is one of the most important skills in English because it affects how professional and clear your communication sounds. When subjects and verbs agree correctly, your sentences flow naturally and your meaning is crystal clear. Whether you're writing an email, giving a presentation, or chatting with friends, proper subject-verb agreement makes you sound confident and educated.

Think about it: when someone says "The students is working hard" instead of "The students are working hard," it immediately sounds wrong to our ears. That's because subject-verb agreement is so fundamental to English that errors jump out at listeners and readers. Mastering this skill will make all of your writing stronger!

By the end of this lesson, you will:

  • Recognize patterns: Identify subject-verb agreement in real sentences and paragraphs

  • Apply confidently: Use correct subject-verb agreement naturally in your own writing
  • Sound professional: Write and speak with the grammar patterns that educated speakers use

✏️ The Golden Rule of Subject-Verb Agreement

📋 The Simple Pattern That Works Every Time

SINGULAR SUBJECT → VERB ENDS WITH -S

Example: The student works hard.


PLURAL SUBJECT → VERB HAS NO -S

Example: The students work hard.

🔍 How to Remember This

Memory trick: In most cases, if you can put "one" in front of the subject, the verb needs an -s. If you can put "many" in front of the subject, the verb doesn't need an -s.

  • "One teacher explains" → The teacher explains
  • "Many teachers explain" → The teachers explain

🗣️ Examples in Real Context

Let's see how subject-verb agreement works in actual writing situations:

📧 Example 1: Professional Email

Context: When writing workplace communications, proper agreement shows professionalism and attention to detail.

"Dear Mr. Johnson,

The meeting starts at 3 PM in the conference room. All employees need to attend this important session. Each department has prepared a brief presentation. The managers will review the quarterly results, and everyone will receive copies of the new policy manual.

Best regards,
Sarah"

Notice: "meeting" (singular) → "starts"; "employees" (plural) → "need"; "department" (singular) → "has"

📖 Example 2: Narrative Writing

Context: In creative writing, smooth subject-verb agreement helps readers focus on your story, not grammar mistakes.

"The old library stands quietly in the center of town. Students study at the wooden tables every afternoon. Each book holds countless stories and adventures. The librarians help visitors find exactly what they need. Everyone loves the peaceful atmosphere that the building provides."

Notice: "library" (singular) → "stands"; "students" (plural) → "study"; "everyone" (always singular) → "loves"

💬 Example 3: Social Media Post

Context: Even in casual online writing, proper agreement makes your posts sound polished and engaging.

"The school play opens tomorrow night! Our talented actors have practiced for months. Every costume looks amazing, and the set design creates a magical atmosphere. All the teachers are excited to see the final performance. Everyone is invited to attend this special event!"

Notice: "play" (singular) → "opens"; "actors" (plural) → "have practiced"; "costume" (singular) → "looks"

🗣️ Speaking Examples: Natural Conversations

Since speaking is where you'll use this skill most often, let's practice with dialogue and conversation patterns you'll actually use:

☕ Scenario 1: Casual Chat with Friends

Context: Even in relaxed conversations, correct grammar sounds natural and educated.

Friend: "How's your family doing?"

You: "Everyone is doing well! My brother works at the new hospital, and my parents are planning a vacation. The whole family meets every Sunday for dinner. My sister brings her children, and they love playing in the garden."

Notice in speech: "everyone" → "is" | "brother" → "works" | "parents" → "are" | "family" → "meets"

📞 Scenario 2: Phone Call (Making Appointments)

Context: Phone calls require clear, correct speech since people can't see your gestures.

Receptionist: "How can I help you?"

You: "I need to schedule a doctor's appointment. My schedule allows morning visits only. Tuesday works best for me. The doctor knows about my condition, and all my records are up to date."

Notice in speech: "I" → "need" | "schedule" → "allows" | "Tuesday" → "works" | "doctor" → "knows"

🏢 Scenario 3: Team Meeting at Work

Context: Professional discussions require clear, accurate grammar to convey ideas effectively.

Manager: "What's the status of the project?"

You: "The team is making good progress. All members have completed their assigned tasks. The deadline approaches quickly, but everyone believes we'll finish on time. Each department contributes important ideas, and the results look very promising."

Notice in speech: "team" → "is" | "members" → "have" | "deadline" → "approaches" | "everyone" → "believes"

🎉 Scenario 4: Social Gathering

Context: Social events are perfect for practicing natural, flowing speech patterns.

New acquaintance: "This is a nice party!"

You: "Yes! The host organizes wonderful events. All the guests seem to be enjoying themselves. The music creates a great atmosphere, and the food tastes delicious. Everyone appears relaxed and happy. These parties happen every month."

Notice in speech: "host" → "organizes" | "guests" → "seem" | "music" → "creates" | "everyone" → "appears"

🎯 Speaking Tips for Subject-Verb Agreement

  • Slow down slightly: When speaking, take your time to think about the subject before choosing the verb
  • Practice common phrases: Memorize patterns like "Everyone is..." and "The team works..."
  • Listen to yourself: If something sounds wrong when you say it, it probably is
  • Use simple subjects first: Start with clear, obvious subjects before tackling complex sentences
  • Record yourself practicing: Listen back to catch patterns you want to improve

❌ Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

🚫 Mistake #1: Confusing Collective Nouns

❌ Wrong: "The team are practicing today."

✅ Right: "The team is practicing today."

Why: Words like "team," "class," "family," and "group" are collective nouns. Even though they refer to multiple people, they act as one unit, so they take singular verbs.

🚫 Mistake #2: Getting Confused by "Of" Phrases

❌ Wrong: "Each of the students have finished their project."

✅ Right: "Each of the students has finished their project."

Why: The subject is "each," not "students." The phrase "of the students" is just extra information. "Each" is always singular, so it needs "has."

🚫 Mistake #3: Indefinite Pronoun Confusion

❌ Wrong: "Everyone are excited about the field trip."

✅ Right: "Everyone is excited about the field trip."

Why: Words like "everyone," "someone," "anyone," and "nobody" are always singular, even though they might refer to many people. They always take singular verbs.

✅ Summary & Key Takeaways

🎯 What You've Learned

  • The Core Rule: Singular subjects take verbs with -s; plural subjects take verbs without -s
  • Context Matters: Subject-verb agreement appears in all types of writing, from emails to stories
  • Watch for Tricky Cases: Collective nouns, "of" phrases, and indefinite pronouns have special rules
  • Trust Your Ear: Correct agreement usually sounds right when you read it aloud
  • Practice Makes Perfect: The more you notice these patterns, the more automatic they become

💡 Pro Tip: When you're unsure about a sentence, try removing any phrases between the subject and verb. This will help you see the core subject-verb relationship more clearly.

Example: "The box [of old photographs] sits on the shelf." Remove the phrase → "The box sits on the shelf." ✓
